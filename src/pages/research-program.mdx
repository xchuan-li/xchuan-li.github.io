# The program

<p class="text-xl font-medium tracking-tight text-[var(--color-text-muted)] -mt-2 mb-3">One question, one method, and the instruments I expect to outlive. Modality is the first case study, not the boundary.</p>

<p class="text-sm text-[var(--color-text-dim)] mb-8"><em>Xiaochuan Li. Working draft for xchuan-li.github.io</em></p>

In one sentence: **I study how linguistic structure becomes representation, and how representation becomes reasoning.** The method is to turn theories of linguistic meaning into predictions that evidence can adjudicate: I bring the structural view of formal semantics to what a model has actually represented, and ask whether its reasoning is licensed by that structure or only correlated with it. The everyday label is *formal, linguistically-informed analysis of language models*; the durable description is the method one layer up. This is not a list of projects but a single program, studied one case study at a time. This page is the longer version: what the work is, why it is mine, and where it goes past modality. The current case study lives on the [research page](/research).

## Four layers, ordered by how long they last

The same work can be described at four heights. Only the top two are my identity. The bottom two are how I happen to reach it this decade, and I expect to outlive them. The mistake I spent years not seeing: an academic identity fused to layer three or four ages with the technology.

**Lifelong: the question.** Does linguistic structure actually carry the meaning our theories assign it? When a theory says a modal word quantifies over a set of worlds, or a quantifier is monotone in its restrictor, it is making a claim about what a system *represents*. The permanent question is whether that structure is really there, doing the work, or whether the behaviour is only correlated with it. This does not age, and it is not about any particular system, whether a child, a model, or a mind.

**Decade-stable: the method.** Turn a theory of meaning into predictions that can be **adjudicated by evidence rather than settled by argument**: state, in advance, what observable trace the theory, *if it were true*, would have to leave behind. This is Kratzer's real move, and the one I take as a model. Her contribution was less the modal base itself than the map she drew from *if modals work like this* to *these language facts must follow*. My method only widens the range of admissible traces, whether a model's internal representations, agreement across languages, or human behaviour, but the shape is hers, and it is stable across whatever tools the decade brings.

**A few years: the experimental strategy.** How I currently produce those verdicts: mechanistic interpretation, causal intervention, comparison across computational and cognitive systems. Today this is the best way to make a theory's trace visible. It is also the layer I most expect to be replaced: by provable representation correspondence, by neural recording, by methods not yet named. When it turns over, the method above does not move.

**Swappable: the tools.** Sparse autoencoders, activation patching, probes, a subspace, a particular model. Implementation. Every item here can be exchanged tomorrow without disturbing anything above it.

Two things this leaves fixed, wherever the instruments go. The apparatus stays an **instrument, not the object**. I am not here to adjudicate the correct analysis of a construction. That is theoretical semantics' honorable work, and it isn't mine. I want the theory sharp because a sharp theory makes a sharp measurement. And the eyesight is the **moat**: carrying the structural view, I see a modal base, a monotonicity profile, the difference between an inference a sentence *licenses* and a cue that merely travels with the answer, where the standard question sees only right-or-wrong behaviour. That difference in what is *seen*, not a new gadget, is what is mine.

## Why modality is the first case study

A program this broad needs one place where its central cut is both *sharp* and *decidable*. Modality is it: necessity and possibility are quantification over alternative worlds; the structure is formally exact; it meets language through a small, well-understood apparatus; and an adjacent field has already built the tasks that separate *behaving as if* from *possessing*. So the first case study is **modal concepts in language models**, my current project and thesis. But modality is the entrance, not the ceiling; the same discipline extends to the **monotonicity of quantifiers**, the **restrictor structure of conditionals**, and on. The method below is stated for the modal case because that is where I stand; its shape is general.

### The method, inherited down one line

The instruments I use to ask the modal question were each forged at a different point on one continuous line, and each hands the next a tool.

- **Leibniz: possibility as structure.** Necessity and possibility are quantification: the necessary holds in *all* worlds, the possible in *some*. Modality becomes structure. (My philosophy thesis was on his *characteristica universalis*, a notation whose form *is* the structure of thought. The distance from there to here is short.)
- **Kripke: the structure formalized.** Possible worlds became mathematics: a frame of worlds and an accessibility relation, with □ and ◇ evaluated against it. The permanent lesson: *an informal capacity becomes testable only once someone states the structure that would license it.* That is the criterion layer of everything I do.
- **Kratzer: modality meets natural language.** Modal words are thin; context supplies a **modal base** (which worlds are quantified over) and an **ordering source** (how they rank). The control knob: hold the word fixed, vary the modal base, and see whether behavior tracks the *word* or the *world-set*, since only the latter is evidence of structure. And because languages lexicalize modality so differently (*may/must* vs. *können/müssen/dürfen* vs. 可能/必须/一定), cross-lingual comparison is a natural experiment I am placed to run.
- **Leahy & Carey: possession versus performance.** Developmental psychology answered the question with a *discriminative methodology*: design tasks a minimal representation **must fail**, where only genuine modal structure licenses the right response. Passing is not possessing. That task logic is the verdict procedure I import.

Modality is the instance where this whole apparatus is easiest to see run. The emblem below is it, live: the ◇/□ verdict of a modal claim read off the *space of alternatives* rather than the word. It is the same "read the verdict off the structure" mechanic the homepage states in general, here in its sharpest special case.

## Two phases, and the traffic between them

- **Phase one, diagnosis (now through the early PhD).** Is the formal-semantic structure actually inside these models, and in what form? Modality first (the pilot and thesis), then quantification, then conditionals. Safe, publishable, cumulative: it draws the map.
- **Phase two, construction (later).** Use the map to *strengthen* the grounding of reasoning in that structure. What is worth building depends on what the diagnosis finds, which is why phase one is a precondition, not a tax. Each diagnostic project leaves a note toward it: *to strengthen this structure's grip on reasoning, what would this result point me to build?* It does not build it yet.

The traffic runs both ways. This is not linguistics validating itself on a new corpus. A model is also an instrument pointed back at the theory, telling us which formal structures are *load-bearing* for a system with only language to go on, and which are ornament. When a model must have a structure to succeed, that is evidence about the structure, not only the model.

## One boundary I keep in view

Where the program is most attackable: reading a null result as *language is insufficient, something must be built in* only holds if the model's input carries modal information comparable to a learner's. Otherwise a failure measures a thin corpus, not a limit of language. Calibrating that baseline is its own question, and where a developmental collaboration enters as load-bearing rather than ornamental.

The through-line: **I study when a system's success is licensed by structure and when it is merely correlated with it, and I want, eventually, to make the licensing real where it is only mimicked.** Leibniz asked it of the perfect language; Kripke of logic; Kratzer of natural language; Carey of children. I ask it of language models.

## Appendix: reading the lineage systematically

A working syllabus, ordered so each stage equips the next. (Personal study plan; folded into the formal-semantics block of my broader curriculum.)

**Stage 1: Modal logic (the criteria layer)**
- Hughes & Cresswell, *A New Introduction to Modal Logic*: chapters 1 to 3 for working fluency (K, T, S4, S5; Kripke frames; axiom-to-frame correspondence).
- Optional depth: Blackburn, de Rijke & Venema, *Modal Logic* (ch. 1); SEP entry "Modal Logic" as a map.

**Stage 2: Modality in natural language (the linguistic layer)**
- Kratzer, "The Notional Category of Modality" (1981; revised in *Modals and Conditionals*, 2012): modal base, ordering source, the thin semantics of modal words.
- von Fintel, "Modality and Language" (survey) and von Fintel & Heim, *Intensional Semantics*: the fastest rigorous entry.
- Portner, *Modality* (Oxford Surveys in Semantics): the systematic treatment, including cross-linguistic variation.

**Stage 3: Modal concepts in development (the verdict layer)**
- Leahy & Carey, "The Acquisition of Modal Concepts," *TiCS* 2020: the minimal/modal distinction and the task logic built on it.
- Carey, *The Origin of Concepts* (2009), ch. 1 & final chapter: conceptual change, which the modal work instantiates.
- Quilty-Dunn, Porot & Mandelbaum, "The Best Game in Town," *BBS* 2023: the representational-format debate in its current form.

**Stage 4: The question posed to language models (the experimental layer)**
- Linzen, "How Can We Accelerate Progress Towards Human-like Linguistic Generalization?" (ACL 2020): the evaluation philosophy this program adopts.
- McCoy, Pavlick & Linzen, "Right for the Wrong Reasons" (ACL 2019): surface heuristics vs. licensing structure.
- Warstadt et al., BabyLM Challenge findings (CoNLL 2023): developmentally-plausible training scales; the bridge back to acquisition.
- Recent work on modal and epistemic reasoning in LMs, tracked via the *Semantics with LMs* literature as it develops.

**Beyond modality: where the same discipline goes next**
- Generalized-quantifier monotonicity (natural logic; the NLI-as-monotonicity line): the next structure to diagnose.
- Conditionals: restrictor semantics (Kratzer) and the structure a model would need to represent one.

**Historical companions (read for pleasure, cite for lineage)**
- Leibniz, "On Freedom and Possibility" and the Arnauld correspondence: possible worlds in their original habitat.
- Kripke, "Semantical Considerations on Modal Logic" (1963): shorter and more readable than its reputation.
