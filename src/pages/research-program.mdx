# The program

<p class="text-xl font-medium tracking-tight text-[var(--color-text-muted)] -mt-2 mb-3">One commitment, four layers, and a long arc — of which modality is the first step, not the boundary.</p>

<p class="text-sm text-[var(--color-text-dim)] mb-8"><em>Xiaochuan Li — working draft for xchuan-li.github.io</em></p>

In one sentence: I do **formal, linguistically-informed analysis of language models** — I bring the structural view of formal semantics to bear on what a language model has actually represented, and whether its reasoning is licensed by that structure or only correlated with it. This page is the longer version. Not the current experiment — that lives on the [research page](/research) — but what the work *is*, why it is mine to do, and where it goes once modality is behind me.

## The commitment, bottom-up

Read from the ground up, the program is four layers. Each one constrains the next, and the top layer is a compass, not a promise.

**I. Linguistics is the root.** My training, my intuitions, and — decisively — my *criteria* come from linguistics: formal semantics and typology. It is the lens I see through and the toolbox I reach into. When I ask whether a model "has" a concept, linguistics is what lets me state in advance what that would even mean — precisely enough that the claim could turn out false. Without a criterion stated first, "the model understands X" is not a finding, it is a mood.

**II. But not pure theory.** I am not going to spend a career adjudicating what the correct analysis of some construction *is*. That is the honorable work of theoretical semantics, and it is not mine. Here the formal apparatus is an **instrument**, not the object of study — and that is exactly the line that separates me from the theoretical linguist I trained beside. I want the theory sharp not for its own sake but because a sharp theory makes a sharp measurement.

**III. The target is language models — and that is the moat.** I turn the structural view of formal semantics outward, onto language models. Because I carry that view, I can see structure where most people working on models see only behavior: a modal base, a monotonicity profile, the difference between an inference a sentence *licenses* and a surface cue that merely travels with the answer. The standard question is *did the model get it right*; the question I am equipped to ask is *is the structure any competent system would need actually in there, and is the answer load-bearing on it?* That difference in eyesight — not a new method, a different thing seen — is the moat.

**IV. The long arc: reasoning grounded in meaning.** Underneath the diagnostic work is a conviction about where it points. When a model reasons and the licensing structure is genuinely present, the reasoning is real; when it is absent, the reasoning is imitation that happens to land. The far horizon is machine reasoning that is **grounded in a semantic representation** rather than floating free of it. I hold this as a *compass, not a route*: you draw the map of what is and is not there before you try to build on it. **Diagnosis is the precondition for construction** — you need the survey of the foundation before you pour anything on top. I am naming the destination so the diagnostic work has a reason to matter, not committing today to a speculative building program whose shape only the diagnosis can decide.

## Why modality is the first instance

A program this broad needs a place where its central cut is at once *sharp* and *decidable*. Modality is that place. Necessity and possibility are quantification over a space of alternative worlds; the structure is formally exact; it meets natural language through a small, well-understood apparatus; and, uniquely, an adjacent field has already built the tasks that separate *behaving as if* from *actually possessing*. So the first instance of the whole program is **modal concepts in language models** — my current project and my Master's thesis. But modality is the entrance, not the ceiling: the same discipline extends to the **monotonicity of quantifiers**, the **restrictor structure of conditionals**, and onward. The method below is stated for the modal case because that is where I am standing; the shape of it is general.

### The method, inherited down one line

The instruments I use to ask the modal question were each forged at a different point on one continuous line — and each hands the next a tool.

- **Leibniz — possibility as structure.** Necessity and possibility are not primitive mysteries but quantification: what is necessary holds in *all* possible worlds, what is possible in *some*. Modality becomes structure. (My undergraduate thesis, in philosophy, was on his *characteristica universalis* — the dream of a notation whose form *is* the structure of thought. The distance from there to here is shorter than it looks.)
- **Kripke — the structure formalized.** Possible worlds became mathematics: a frame of worlds and an accessibility relation, with □ and ◇ evaluated against it. The permanent lesson is methodological — *an informal capacity becomes testable only when someone states the structure that would license it.* This is the criterion layer of everything I do.
- **Kratzer — modality meets natural language.** Modal words are semantically thin; context supplies a **modal base** (which worlds are quantified over) and an **ordering source** (how they are ranked). This hands the experimentalist a control knob: hold the modal word fixed, vary the modal base, and see whether behavior tracks the *word* or the *quantified world-set*. Only the latter is evidence of structure — and because languages lexicalize modality so differently (*may/must* vs. *können/müssen/dürfen* vs. 可能/必须/一定), cross-lingual comparison becomes a natural experiment I am placed to run.
- **Leahy & Carey — possession versus performance.** Developmental psychology inherited the question in its hardest empirical form and answered it with a *discriminative methodology*: design tasks a minimal representation **must fail**, where only genuine modal structure licenses the correct response. Passing is not possessing. Six decades of that task logic is the verdict procedure I import.

## Two phases, and the traffic between them

- **Phase one — diagnosis (now through the early PhD).** Is the formal-semantic structure actually inside these models, and in what form? Modality first (the pilot and thesis), then quantification, then conditionals. This phase is safe, publishable, and cumulative — it is what draws the map.
- **Phase two — construction (later).** Use the map to *strengthen* the grounding of reasoning in that structure. What is worth building depends entirely on what the diagnosis finds, which is why phase one is a precondition and not a tax. Each diagnostic project leaves a note toward it — *if you wanted to strengthen this structure's grip on reasoning, what would this result point you to build?* — without committing to build it yet.

And the traffic runs both directions. This is not linguistics validating itself on a new corpus. A language model is also an instrument pointed back at the theory: it can tell us which formal structures are *load-bearing* for a system that has only language to go on, and which are the theorist's ornament. When the model must have a structure to succeed, that is evidence about the structure, not only about the model.

## One boundary I keep in view

The place this program is most attackable: reading a null result as *language is insufficient, something must be built in* only goes through if the model's linguistic input carries modal information comparable to a learner's — otherwise a failure measures a thin corpus, not a limit of language. Calibrating that baseline — how much modal information natural language actually carries, and in what form — is its own question, and it is where a developmental collaboration enters the program as load-bearing rather than ornamental.

The through-line, stated once: **I study when a system's success is licensed by structure and when it is merely correlated with it — and I want, eventually, to make the licensing real where it is only mimicked.** Leibniz asked the first half of the perfect language; Kripke of logic; Kratzer of natural language; Carey of children. I ask it of language models, and I intend to keep asking until the answer is good enough to build on.

## Appendix: reading the lineage systematically

A working syllabus, ordered so each stage equips the next. (Personal study plan; folded into the formal-semantics block of my broader curriculum.)

**Stage 1 — Modal logic (the criteria layer)**
- Hughes & Cresswell, *A New Introduction to Modal Logic* — chapters 1–3 for working fluency (K, T, S4, S5; Kripke frames; axiom–frame correspondence).
- Optional depth: Blackburn, de Rijke & Venema, *Modal Logic* (ch. 1); SEP entry "Modal Logic" as a map.

**Stage 2 — Modality in natural language (the linguistic layer)**
- Kratzer, "The Notional Category of Modality" (1981; revised in *Modals and Conditionals*, 2012) — modal base, ordering source, the thin semantics of modal words.
- von Fintel, "Modality and Language" (survey) and von Fintel & Heim, *Intensional Semantics* — the fastest rigorous entry.
- Portner, *Modality* (Oxford Surveys in Semantics) — the systematic treatment, including cross-linguistic variation.

**Stage 3 — Modal concepts in development (the verdict layer)**
- Leahy & Carey, "The Acquisition of Modal Concepts," *TiCS* 2020 — the minimal/modal distinction and the task logic built on it.
- Carey, *The Origin of Concepts* (2009), ch. 1 & final chapter — conceptual change, which the modal work instantiates.
- Quilty-Dunn, Porot & Mandelbaum, "The Best Game in Town," *BBS* 2023 — the representational-format debate in its current form.

**Stage 4 — The question posed to language models (the experimental layer)**
- Linzen, "How Can We Accelerate Progress Towards Human-like Linguistic Generalization?" (ACL 2020) — the evaluation philosophy this program adopts.
- McCoy, Pavlick & Linzen, "Right for the Wrong Reasons" (ACL 2019) — surface heuristics vs. licensing structure.
- Warstadt et al., BabyLM Challenge findings (CoNLL 2023) — developmentally-plausible training scales; the bridge back to acquisition.
- Recent work on modal and epistemic reasoning in LMs — tracked via the *Semantics with LMs* literature as it develops.

**Beyond modality — where the same discipline goes next**
- Generalized-quantifier monotonicity (natural logic; the NLI-as-monotonicity line) — the next structure to diagnose.
- Conditionals: restrictor semantics (Kratzer) and the structure a model would need to represent one.

**Historical companions (read for pleasure, cite for lineage)**
- Leibniz, "On Freedom and Possibility" and the Arnauld correspondence — possible worlds in their original habitat.
- Kripke, "Semantical Considerations on Modal Logic" (1963) — shorter and more readable than its reputation.
