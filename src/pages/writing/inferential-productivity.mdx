# Reasoning is generative; grounding needs the other direction

Most of the conceptual machinery we have for reasoning runs in one direction. Premises combine into conclusions; conclusions combine into further conclusions. Closure operators, proof trees, chain-of-thought traces, attention patterns over context — all of them describe an outward motion. Given a state, produce more states. The natural growth rate of this motion is exponential, and in practice the only thing that bounds it is whatever stopping criterion we attach from outside.

I want to call this *inferential productivity*: the rate at which a system, given a structure, can generate new conclusions consistent with it. A high-productivity system is one whose state space fans out quickly under composition. Most contemporary language models are, in this sense, extremely productive.

The thing I keep noticing in my own work is that productivity is not what's hard to get. What's hard to get — and what almost none of our standard tools measure — is the opposite motion. Given a set of conclusions a system arrives at, what is the minimum causal structure that licenses them? Not which structure *can* license them, in the sense of being consistent; but which is the smallest structure that *must* be appealed to for the conclusions to land. Call this the *minimum licensing structure*, in the spirit of Occam's razor and minimum description length, but specifically over causal rather than statistical sufficiency.

These two directions are not symmetric, and that asymmetry is the part I think is doing real work.

Productivity is cheap. Anything that composes — a grammar, a vector space, a transformer layer — has productivity for free. Modern models are by default high-productivity systems; that's what scale gets you. Minimum licensing structure, on the other hand, is expensive. It requires not just generating outputs but explaining which substructure of the model is doing the explanatory work, and showing that the rest is removable without behavioral cost. This is, in the limit, a causal claim, not a statistical one. Stability under perturbation is not enough — a stable answer can still be riding a shortcut, as the SC-spurious case in my first paper makes explicit.

I've come to think of much of the interpretability literature as caught in the productivity direction. A model produces answers; we examine the answers, sometimes the activations behind them, often the steering vectors that move them. What we are usually *not* doing is asking, for a given correct behavior, what the smallest substructure is whose removal would cost the behavior. The honest version of "is this model reasoning?" is not "did it produce the right output" but "is the structure we'd point to as the reason actually load-bearing for the output?". The first question is about productivity; the second is about minimum licensing.

This is the gap that CIY is trying to occupy. The point of a graded inferential-yield measure is not to celebrate models that get more right. It is to discriminate between two models that get the same things right for structurally different reasons — one whose conclusions are licensed by a small, identifiable causal substructure, and one whose conclusions are licensed by everything-correlated-with-everything-else. The falsifiability constraint built into CIY is precisely the worry that, without it, "more grounded reasoning" silently collapses back into "more accuracy" and the asymmetry I'm pointing at gets lost.

I don't think reasoning systems get smarter by becoming more productive. Past a certain point of capability that direction is essentially free. What's not free, and what I think is the actual axis of progress, is the other direction — the discipline of finding, for a productive system's behavior, the smallest causal structure that has to be there.
