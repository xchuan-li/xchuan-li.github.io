# Inverted Observability

## Psychology and interpretability as mirror sciences — and language as the instrument they share

Two fields are asking the same question without knowing they are asking the same question.

Developmental psychology asks: a child passes a task — has she *actually* grasped the concept, or is she getting by on something shallower? Interpretability asks: a model returns the right answer — is it *licensed* by the structure in the sentence that genuinely entails the conclusion, or pushed by a surface shortcut that merely correlates with it?

Strip the vocabulary and it is one question: **behind a system that looks like it "can," what is the format of the representation?** The real structure, or its shadow.

But the two fields stand on opposite sides of that question — opposite sides of a mirror.

### One · The inversion

The difference is not who *has* what. It is who can *trust* what.

On the human side, **behavior is observable and mechanism is not**. You see the child's choice on a false-belief task — clean, repeatable, ecologically valid; you cannot see the representation inside her head. One correction here, because the claim is usually overstated: the brain is not opaque. EEG, fNIRS, and MRI have read human mechanism for decades — which white-matter tract has matured, which time-window lights up — only at *low resolution, by correlation rather than intervention, and almost never by writing to it*. Behavior is the clean window.

On the model side, it is the other way around. **Mechanism is readable and writable**: every activation is laid out in front of you, and you can ablate, steer, patch — the clean causal access neuroscience has always wanted. But **behavior turns cheap and suspect**: the "reasoning" a model emits — is it reasoning, or a correlation copied out of the training data? Its status is unknown.

So the mirror can be put more sharply than "mirror image": **each field trusts what the other cannot see, and is skeptical of what the other anchors on.** Psychology anchors on behavior and treats mechanism as the unknown to be inferred; interpretability anchors on mechanism and treats behavior as the evidence to be doubted.

Developmental neuroscience is the middle rung on the mechanism side of this mirror — it already does "mechanism" on humans, only with the resolution dial turned far down. Interpretability is roughly that dial turned to maximum, with writing allowed.

### Two · What each lends the other

Once the inversion is clear, "the two feed each other" stops being a slogan and becomes a specific methodological trade.

Psychology lends interpretability **a craft, decades in the making, for diagnosing a hidden format from behavior alone**. False belief, the disjunctive syllogism, the minimal-versus-modal designs — every one of them is engineered to decide *which* format a representation has *without being able to see the mechanism*. Interpretability is still clumsily reinventing these: handing a theory-of-mind task to a model and then watching an irrelevant rewording knock the result over is a crude reinvention. At reading format off behavior, developmental psychology is the world champion — and that is exactly what interpretability is short of once its access to mechanism runs out.

Interpretability lends back **a model organism with the mechanism switched on** — a system that can finally be checked against the answer key. A behavioral paradigm asserts "this performance must require some structured representation"; you used to have no way to verify it. Now you can go into the model and look — whether the representation is there, whether it is used, where. It is a ground-truth oracle psychology never had.

### Three · Where the mirror lies

But mirrors deceive, and this is the place to be careful.

The sharpest objection runs: a superposed "causal-direction" feature inside a model — what gives it the right to tell you anything about a child's mind? The objection is correct. Both systems represent "causal direction," but that may be a *convergent solution to the same task* rather than a *shared mechanism* — a bird's wing and an insect's wing both fly without being the same organ.

So the trade has to be graded by layer. At the level of *what to ask, and how to diagnose format from behavior*, it is strong. At the level of *what the mechanism actually is*, it is risky. Treating a model as a model organism is meant in the biologist's sense — it is useful *because* it is manipulable; but the homology has to be *argued*, not *assumed*.

This is the same discipline I keep returning to elsewhere — [claim only where it is identifiable](/research/sc-certification). Cross-system mechanistic sameness: until you can earn it, stop honestly at "convergent," and do not dress it up as "homologous."

### Four · The instrument they share

Between the two sides of the mirror runs one main road, and it is language.

The reason is plain, and I think it is the whole point: language is **the one instrument both sides can read**. Human "reasoning behavior" is largely linguistic — modal words, causal connectives, the false-belief story told out loud; and a language model *is made of* language. Language is therefore the single substrate on which the human side and the model side become *commensurable*: the same causal sentence can be put, unchanged, to a child and to a model.

My [Causal Direction](/research/causal-direction) pilot works for exactly this reason — "the storm caused the flood" and its reverse are *at once* a psychological stimulus and an interpretability probe. That is not a coincidence; it is the nature of the road. It is also why the person best placed to stand on this bridge tends to be one trained in linguistics and philosophy, rather than a specialist of either side alone.

### Five · The bridge's foundation is itself a thing to be tested

Here I am tempted to write the elegant line: **language carries thought, but language is not thought.** It would make the whole bridge look self-evident.

But it is not the bridge's foundation. It is a contested claim with a side. The language-of-thought camp supports it — thought has its own, non-linguistic format, and language only expresses it. Linguistic relativity, and the developmental line on which *language transforms* implicit reasoning into explicit reasoning, push back — language is not a mere carrier; it *restructures* the thought. For a language model the matter is wide open and empirical: in a system that is *only* language, is there any "thought" separable from language at all?

And that is the first thing this bridge lets me *test*. In the pilot, a pure-language model holds a causal direction abstract enough to transfer to *invented*, zero-frequency relations — evidence that even inside a thing made of language, there is some structure that is thought-like and not purely linguistic.

So "language is not thought" is not the floor I stand on. It is the proposition the bridge puts within reach. I do not need to *settle* it from the armchair; I need its *workable* weaker form, and one honest sentence about when the judgment is decidable and when it is not.

The mirror, the mutual lending, the shared road — they are all in service of one thing: moving a question that could only be argued in philosophy for two thousand years to a place where you can read both the behavior and the mechanism, and test it for the first time.
