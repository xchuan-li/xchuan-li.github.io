# Why interpretability needs a decidability boundary

There is a difference between a measurement being hard and a quantity being undefined. The
interpretability literature mostly assumes the first; I think much of it is in fact the second,
past a certain depth.

This post is a short version of an argument I am working out in more rigorous form. It has three
moves.

## 1. Stability is not grounding

Most "robustness" or "consistency" checks ask: does the model's answer survive an answer-preserving
perturbation? This is a real check, and it catches a real failure mode (the model that flips its
answer under paraphrase). But it is shallower than people read it as. A model can be perfectly
stable and perfectly accurate on a slice and still be reading entirely the wrong feature — as long
as that feature happens to track the label on this evaluation distribution.

I have a [demo of this](/research/sc-certification) that lets you flip the regime and watch the
verdict invert. The numbers are calibrated to a real experiment; the point is that they don't have
to be — the structural argument runs first.

## 2. The cut that resolves it requires authorship

If you want to ask "is this model riding a shortcut?" in a way that is not itself a judgment call,
you need two things at once: a benchmark where the ground-truth causal graph is stipulated *by
construction*, and a model whose training correlations are *auditable*. Either alone is
insufficient. Both together let you run a do-intervention that severs a specific candidate
shortcut, paired with a negative control that rules out "any change breaks it."

The crucial thing — and the part that I think gets missed — is that this is not a technique that
gets sharper as your instruments improve. It is a question that is only well-posed in the
construction-and-auditability regime. In the wild, the cut is not hard; it is **non-identifiable
from the available observations**.

## 3. The honest move is to stop

On a real benchmark with an opaque-pretrained model, the honest decomposition stops at the
level-two cut: unstable / stable-wrong / stable-correct. The deeper cut — "of these
stable-corrects, how many are causally grounded versus stably riding a shortcut?" — is not
something you can report. Reporting it anyway is not a stronger result; it is an underdetermined
one.

I think this is uncomfortable but useful. It reframes a recurring pattern in interpretability
debates: claims about "genuine reasoning" on natural benchmarks are contested not because the
measurement is noisy but because, past a certain depth, there is no identifiable quantity to
measure without the construction.
