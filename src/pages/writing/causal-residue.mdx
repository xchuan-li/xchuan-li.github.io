# Causal structure as residue — training as elimination

A short note putting down what I think is the hidden unification of the research
program I'm working on. It came out of a conversation and I'm writing it here
partly to see whether it survives being written.

Causal structure is not produced by a model's correct answers; it is what
remains after competing explanations have been eliminated.

We usually frame *learning a causal structure* as the productive direction: data
goes in, structure comes out. The picture comes prepackaged with supervised
learning — input on the left, output on the right, a learner in the middle, and
the question is how well the inferred structure agrees with the generator.
The work is in the inference step.

I want to flag a different framing. It is older than ML — it has a lineage that
runs through Bacon, Mill, Popper, and Mitchell's version-space learning — but it
has been quiet at the ML training-loop level even when the underlying algorithms
are doing it. The framing is **eliminative**: a causal structure is what is left
after the candidates have been ruled out. The work is not in producing a
structure; it is in cutting away the structures that are no longer compatible
with the behavior we have seen.

## The reframe

Each training example is, on this view, not principally a `(input, label)` pair.
It is a **falsifier** — a behavioral observation that is compatible with some
causal structures and incompatible with others. Each example partitions the
hypothesis space into the structures that remain live and the ones that this
datum has ruled out.

The structure the learner "ends up with" is the residue of cumulative cutting.
Training is not "compute a structure from data"; it is "shrink the hypothesis
space until what remains is small enough to be informative."

This is, of course, the picture that constraint-based causal discovery (PC, FCI)
has had for decades. It is also the picture behind active learning's notion of a
version space. What I think is missing is the framing being made explicit at the
ML *training-loop* level — at the level of how we describe what a transformer
"learns" from a dataset. The loop runs over a sequence of examples and what it
should be reporting is not an opaque parameter vector that happened to fit, but a
*constrained subset of structures consistent with the cumulative behavior*.

## Why this fits this research program

Looking back at the research program on this site, the core sequence and its
extensions are all instances of elimination:

- [**Stable Is Not Grounded**](/research/sc-certification) is a single structured
  falsifier. The `do`-intervention takes a candidate hypothesis — *this
  particular shortcut is the load-bearing structure* — and either kills it or
  fails to kill it. The matched negative control is what rules out "any
  intervention breaks it"; without that, the cut is not clean.
- [**Isotrace**](/research/isotrace) is a sequence of behavioral falsifiers, one
  per hop, on the hypothesis that compositional reasoning is licensing this
  answer. Where the falsifier fires localizes where the substitution happens.
- [**CIY**](/research/ciy) is the graded relaxation of elimination: not
  *rule the structure in or out* but *how much of the licensing structure has
  actually been used*.
- [**MiniCausalLang**](/research/mini-causal-models) is the eliminative
  process in sandbox form: a synthetic environment where the ground-truth
  structure is planted, training data is generated from it, and recovery can be
  scored against a known target.

I had not previously framed these as one operation with a core sequence and
supporting infrastructure. I think that is the right shape.

## Four pressures the framing has to survive

The framing is not free; if it is to do real work it has to be implemented
against several genuine pressures.

**1. The hypothesis space needs a prior.** Pure elimination over "all directed
acyclic graphs on n nodes" is intractable; the number of DAGs explodes
super-exponentially. Any usable eliminative training loop has to start with a
hypothesis space that already encodes substantial structural priors — grammar,
type, locality, sparsity. The interesting question is what the *minimal* prior is
that still admits identifiable structure recovery from behavior alone.

**2. "Each example is a label" is the wrong analogy.** Standard supervised
learning is `(input, label)`. Eliminative learning is closer to active learning's
version-space picture: each example is not a target but a falsifier. The
bookkeeping the learner needs to do is not "minimize prediction loss" but "track
which structures remain compatible." The closest practical analogue may be data
labeling, *if* you let the "label" be "the subset of structures this example is
consistent with" — but that is a different object from a class label, and
collapsing the two is what makes the eliminative framing invisible in standard
training loops.

**3. Binary elimination conflicts with graded measurement.** Strict elimination
is in or out. [CIY](/research/ciy) is graded. If the training loop is binary you
lose CIY's resolution; if it is graded, it is closer to a Bayesian posterior
update over structures. There is a real choice here, and I think the right one
is the graded version with binary elimination as the limiting case — but the
binary version is the one that has a clean falsifiability check, so it cannot
just be abandoned.

**4. Underdetermination is not a bug, it is a theorem.** Different structures
can produce identical observational distributions; the Markov equivalence class
is exactly this fact. Pure observation can never eliminate within an equivalence
class, but intervention can. The SC project's commitment to `do`-interventions
is not just methodological taste; it is the only way the deeper cut becomes
well-posed at all. An eliminative training loop that uses only observational
data has a non-empty residue *by construction*, and the residue is the
equivalence class — not the single planted structure.

## A concrete next step

The [MiniCausalLang](/research/mini-causal-models) workbench is the natural
test bed. The experiment is to implement a training loop that explicitly logs
hypothesis-space cuts per example: at step *t*, the model's behavior is
compatible with hypothesis set *H<sub>t</sub> ⊆ H<sub>t-1</sub>*. Two things have
ground truth in this setup, because the truth is *planted*:

- The convergence rate of *|H<sub>t</sub>|* toward the planted-truth equivalence class.
- The residual underdetermination after the data runs out — how much of the
  equivalence class is left when the budget of falsifiers is exhausted.

Both have ground truth because the truth is planted. The recovery method and the
validation share a single sandbox. This is the move I keep trying to make in
this research line — taking a methodological intuition and putting it on a
sandbox where it can be evaluated against a planted answer, before it has to
survive contact with a real model.

If the result holds, the unification is real: the work is not a bundle of
parallel projects, but one causal-licensing program with a core sequence,
supporting language-to-causal-graph workbench, and extensions.
