# From a Sufficient Reason to a Universal Language

## What philosophy gave me that ML didn't

My undergraduate thesis was on Leibniz. Specifically, on the *characteristica universalis* — Leibniz's lifelong project to construct a universal symbolic system in which every concept would be a calculable expression, every inference a calculation, and disputes between philosophers would be resolved by sitting down together and saying *calculemus*, let us calculate. The thesis traced this ambition through Leibniz's combinatorial, arithmetic, and geometric attempts, the early predicate logic that grew out of it, the **principle of sufficient reason**, and the long downstream arc through Frege, Russell, and the founding of modern mathematical logic.

I did not, at the time, think of it as research relevant to AI. It was the philosophy of language and logic. It was about Leibniz. It was finished, and then I went to do other things.

What I notice now — two years and a discipline change later — is that the work that has come to occupy me at UTN is Leibniz's ambition split into two poles: a question I can actually act on, and a goal I can only move toward. The **operative question** is the principle of sufficient reason — *nihil est sine ratione*, nothing is without a reason — put to a model: when it answers correctly, what is the sufficient reason? The genuine structure that licenses the answer, or a surface accident that merely correlates with it? The **horizon** is the *characteristica universalis* itself — a language in which surface and structure coincide, where that question could not even arise. Three threads run between the question and the goal.

### One · The question — a sufficient reason

<LeibnizParallelFigure />

Underneath Leibniz's whole project sits a single principle: *nihil est sine ratione* — for everything that is the case, there is a sufficient reason why it is so and not otherwise. His bet was that the disorderly outside world could be *reduced* in light of it — first to a small inventory of conceptual primitives, then to symbolic representations of those primitives, then to a calculus that operated on those representations and produced new symbolic states that, decoded, would correspond to true claims about the world. The bet was reductive in a specific sense: it claimed that you could, in principle, get from arbitrary natural-language disagreement to a finite formal procedure whose outputs settled the disagreement. Calculation as the terminal court of appeal.

He never finished the project. Almost no one thinks any more that the strong form of it is achievable. But what the thesis spent a long time on, in the back of my head, was not whether the project succeeded — it was the structural question that the principle *poses*: under what conditions is the reason even checkable? When you say "this concept maps to this symbol," when can that mapping be verified, rather than asserted? When you say "this conclusion follows from these premises," when is that a fact about the world, and when is it an artifact of the encoding?

Those questions stayed with me. They turned out to be the same questions I needed when I started reading the interpretability literature.

---

The contemporary version goes like this. We have a model. The model produces outputs. We want to know whether the outputs are *licensed* by something we'd recognize as the right structure — the underlying task, the causal graph of the data, the genuine concept — or whether they are produced by something else, a shortcut or a correlation, that happens to be aligned with the right structure on the slice we're testing. In Leibniz's vocabulary: does the answer have a sufficient *grounding* reason, or only a sufficient *predictive* one?

This is, almost exactly, the principle of sufficient reason recast as a measurement problem. The "right structure" plays the role of the genuine reason; the model's behavior plays the role of the consequence; and the question "is the behavior licensed by the structure?" is just "what is the sufficient reason for this answer?". The difference is that we have, today, an actual tool — interventional causal inference — for asking it with some rigor. Where Leibniz had to gesture at the form of the reduction, we can stipulate a causal graph by construction and perform <code>do</code>-interventions to test which substructures are actually load-bearing.

But the deeper continuity is in what *cannot* be done. What I find genuinely interesting is that the sufficient reason is not always *recoverable*. Deciding it requires what I call two-sided observability: the underlying causal structure has to be stipulated by construction, and the model's training distribution has to be auditable. Outside that regime, the grounding question is not hard — it is *non-identifiable from the available observations*. There is no measurement procedure that returns the right answer, because the quantity being measured is not pinned down by the data.

This is, at root, the same structural lesson Leibniz's project teaches. The reduction is not a universally available operation. There are conditions under which symbols faithfully encode concepts, and conditions under which they do not, and the latter is the generic case. What's hard is not the calculus. What's hard is securing the conditions under which the calculus is doing anything at all.

### Two · The goal — a meaning beneath the languages

The second recasting is no longer the question I test but the goal I am walking toward — and it sits even closer to what Leibniz actually wanted. The *characteristica universalis* was never only a calculus; it was a bet about **language**. Leibniz wanted a *real character* whose symbols mirrored concepts directly, beneath the accidents of Latin, German, or Chinese — a representation of meaning that did not depend on which natural language you happened to think in. Frege later gave this a sharp form: the *thought* a sentence expresses is meant to be the same whatever language carries it. Wittgenstein and Quine pushed the other way — meaning is use, and translation is indeterminate; perhaps there is no language-independent content to recover, only the surface practices of each particular tongue. This is the part of analytic philosophy of language I spent the most time in, and at the time it felt like a closed historical debate.

A multilingual model turns that four-century argument into something I can actually probe. The same meaning is carried by German morphology, English word order, and Chinese context; the question is whether the model represents a structure *beneath* those forms — a Leibnizian character, a Fregean thought — or whether it is, as Quine suspected, riding the surface of each language. That is exactly the [typological grounding](/research/typological-grounding) question, and it is the sufficient-reason question once more, one layer over: is there a language-independent structure the behavior is licensed by — and, just as before, when is that even decidable?

<LeibnizLanguageFigure />

A genuine *characteristica* would be the limit of this: a language whose surface transparently carried its structure, so that no model could ride the surface without also tracking the structure — there would be nothing else to ride. Natural language is the opposite, surface and structure pulled apart, which is precisely what makes a shortcut possible. I am not building the universal character. But the fully-auditable synthetic tasks I build are miniature ones — small languages compiled from a known graph, where the map from surface to structure is stipulated — and they are how I keep the sufficient-reason question decidable while reaching, by inches, toward the harder case where the structure is a real language's.

### Three · Freedom inside determinism

The third thread I only connected later. Alongside the logic, my thesis kept circling Leibniz's *determinism* — and the strange compatibilist freedom he carves out inside it. For Leibniz you are free not because you could have done otherwise, but when you become, unimpeded, what your own nature determines you to become; freedom is a matter of adequate understanding, not of an open future. Determined — but by what is genuinely yours, rather than by an external accident.

That is almost exactly the distinction my work turns on. A model's answer is always determined by *something*; the question is whether it is licensed by the genuine structure — its own, in the relevant sense — or pushed by a surface cue that merely happens to correlate. Grounded behavior is "free" in Leibniz's sense: determined by the structure that should govern it. Spurious behavior is the unfree kind — determined from outside, by the accident of a shortcut. The same compatibilist move, relocated from a metaphysics of persons to the mechanics of a model — and it is why "what is the sufficient reason?" and "is the behaviour its own?" are, here, the same question.

I would not have known to look for this lesson if I hadn't spent a year reading Leibniz. The ML literature, as a literature, does not foreground this distinction. It tends to treat "is this model reasoning?" as a measurement problem with a noisy answer, rather than as a question whose well-posedness depends on observational conditions that may or may not obtain. Coming to it from philosophy — specifically from the part of philosophy that has spent four centuries trying to figure out what symbolic systems can and cannot do — made the *non-identifiability* part visible, where the typical ML training would have made it invisible.

This is, I think, the answer to a question I sometimes get from people who notice the trajectory: why philosophy first? Was it useful? It was useful in two ways that are hard to substitute for. The first is training in noticing the difference between a hard measurement and an undefined quantity — Leibniz, Frege, the whole logical-empiricism debate is precisely about this distinction. The second is tolerance for sitting with conclusions you cannot yet ground. ML, as a field, rewards speed and benchmarks. Philosophy rewards being slow about what your claims actually commit you to. Both are real skills, and the second one turns out to matter a lot when you are trying to do interpretability in a way that is not just confident pattern-matching.

The project I'm working on now is not Leibniz's project. The ambition is smaller, and the tools are different: the principle of sufficient reason is a question I can put to a model today, while the *characteristica universalis* stays a horizon I only move toward. But the line between them — *under what conditions can a system's behavior be reduced to a minimum structure that licenses it?* — is Leibniz's line, and the disciplinary continuity is real. I owe the framing to a year I spent on a senior thesis I did not, at the time, think had anything to do with my future.
